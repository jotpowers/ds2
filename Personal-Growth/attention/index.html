attention

Conventential wisdow is that the most valuable thing you have is time. It's a one-way arrow that never points backwards. And that's correct, but not true.

Let's consider the most obvious example: Video conference calls.

A 30-minute conference call can either involve you sitting there with your camera off and on-mute, or you can be on the call, on video and giving it your full attention.

Those two things take the exact same amount of time, but they have vastly different expenditures of attention.

"Great," you say, "so what?"

The key to this is to recognize, instinctively, we all measure the worth of something by the amount of attention that it receives. So, when you have things that you want to accomplish, everyone is assessing how much of your attention you are spending on it.

So, let's talk about the project that is your "Number One Priority."

"This is a top priority for me and the company! It's so important I've assigned Bob, assistant manager to the manager of the south eastern region."

Everyone will look at this statement and observe the subsequent actions.

If you're part of the quarterly review, where Bob provides the powerpoint update, to you and your staff, it gets assigned a relatively low attention score.

However, if you have weekly status calls with Bob and the team, where you are engaged and asking questions, it will be assigned a high attention score.

Importantly, that means it is more likely to be successful because you have chosen to spend your limited attention on it.

"Yes, but I can't afford to spend every week being engaged in these status meetings!"

100% true, and that's what makes attention so valuable. You can't, and every human does instinctive calculus on this.

So, if theyr'e doing that, it behooves you to be deliberate about how you're spending that coin.

This is one of the reasons that 1-on-1s are so valuable. Regardless as to what you talk about or accomplish, you are choosing to spend time that you could spend a lot of different places, on a single person. You are demonstrating to them their value by spending something that you could spend with a whole group of people on a project, on a single person.

Maybe this isn't how you think about these things, but it's to your detriment.
